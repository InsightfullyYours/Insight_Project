The Causes of Infection

We have previously looked at both the scale and demographics of the AIDS epidemic in the U.S. between January 1981 and December 2002. In broad strokes, the AIDS epidemic was diagnosed primarily in individuals between 25 and 44 years in age. Further investigation into the changing diagnosis age range with year indicated that there were interesting changes worth further study. There was a broadening in the age range of diagnosis around 1992, with that same group ageing into the most prevalent age group for diagnoses 5-10 years later. Pediatric cases also started to be diagnosed beginning around 1985, and only decreased again around 1997. In this section we investigate these changes by looking at the mechanisms by which diagnosed patients were exposed to HIV/AIDS.

Figure 5 graphs the number of diagnoses against the mechanism of transmission of the HIV virus for all years in the database. The primary documented transmission route during the AIDS crisis was male homosexual/bisexual contact, supporting our initial discussion about the impact of AIDS on the gay community of the 1990s. There were 859,000 diagnoses in the database; about half of them were due to male homosexual/bisexual contact. That is an enormous number of individuals to be concentrated in a comparatively small community. The second transmission route is IV drug use, followed by heterosexual transmission of HIV. This appears to explain the primary age ranges of infection – 25-44 year olds are at heightened risk of sexually transmitted diseases and are the primary users of IV drugs. The bottom graph of Figure 5, with its logarithmic y-axis, again highlights some interesting numbers.

Blood transfusions to adults and people with hemophiliac/coagulation disorders were a significant source of secondary infection during the crisis. AIDS transmission through blood transfusion was first reported by the CDC on December 10, 1982, very early in the AIDS crisis. Widespread screening of the U.S. blood bank supplies did not begin until March 2, 1985 when the first commercial blood test was licensed. Pediatric transmission was primarily from the mother, as the numbers of pediatric transfusion cases were very small. These differing rates may be due to the relative prevalence of mothers with HIV compared to the rate of administered pediatric transfusions.



We again break the transmission method down by diagnosis year in order to more fully explore the numbers in Figure 5. Figure 6 separates the HIV transmission method down by year of diagnosis. The top plot has a linear z-scale, while the bottom plot has a logarithmic z-scale in order to highlight more subtle evolutions in the method of HIV transmission with time. The top plot confirms that there were additional cases diagnosed between 1992 and 1995 that may correspond to the widening of the age distribution of cases seen previously in Figure 4. The additional cases are due to new transmission mechanisms that were, in general, not identified at large scales previous to 1992: IV drug use by heterosexual men and women, IV drug use by homo/bisexual men, and heterosexual contact with HIV. The bottom graph shows those mechanisms were present as early as 1985, but as a much smaller fraction of diagnosed cases. Comparing the two graphs indicates that much of the increase in diagnoses was due to IV drug use by heterosexual men and women. This is most likely due to the relative number of people in each population. This highlights the importance of the needle exchange programs that began during those years – they may be one of the primary reasons for the decrease in HIV transmission through IV drug use from 1997 on. The bottom plot also confirms that the major pediatric transmission source was the mother, most likely during pregnancy.



We further confirm the above conclusions by comparing the HIV transmission method to the age at diagnosis. This is shown in Figure 7, confirming both the narrow age ranges affected as well as the prevalence of male homosexual/bisexual contact as the transmission source. This analysis confirms and highlights that the gay community was the group primarily affected by the AIDS crisis of the late 20th century.
