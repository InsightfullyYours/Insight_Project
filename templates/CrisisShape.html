The Shape of the AIDS Crisis

We begin the analysis with a broad overview of the AIDS crisis. The section of the database we are exploring includes 859,000 diagnoses, or about 41,000 diagnoses per month. The true shape of the crisis can be seen in Figure 1, which plots the number of AIDS diagnoses in America by month between January 1981 and December 2002. The initial spike at 1981 is because the public database aggregates all the 1981 diagnoses to preserve the anonymity of the 270 initial victims. The rapid decrease in the plot at the end of 2002 is not due to a drop in diagnoses, but instead the termination of data aggregation for the database.



The shape of the AIDS crisis is evident in Figure 1. An initial increase to about 1000 diagnoses per month by 1985, followed by a more rapid increase and maximum rate of diagnoses at the end of 1992 and early 1993. From there, there is a decrease in the rate of AIDS diagnoses. Despite the decrease in the rate of new diagnoses shown in Figure 1, the total number of AIDS diagnoses in the U.S. continued to grow at an alarming rate, as shown in Figure 2. The 859,000 diagnoses captured in this section of the database constitute more than of the entire Population of Alaska or Wyoming in 2014.
